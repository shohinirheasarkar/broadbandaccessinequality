Who Has Access to the Internet?

As we discussed in class, many people describe broadband internet as a basic utility. It is essential for work, education, healthcare, and civic participation. Yet access to reliable, and high-speed internet varies significantly across income levels and geographic regions in the United States. These disparities, commonly referred to as the digital divide, reflect inequalities in which communities get infrastructure investment, and how that affects their daily lives.

Source: U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey (ACS) 2023; National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA)

Geography Shapes Connectivity

Geography plays a central role in determining broadband access. Rural and sparsely populated regions face higher per-household infrastructure costs, making private investment less profitable. Additionally, despite bills being passed to help combat this, only the bare minimum is done to provide internet access. As a result, residents in these areas are more likely to rely on slower connections or lack access altogether, even when broadband service is technically available elsewhere in the state.

Source: Federal Communications Commission (FCC) Broadband Deployment Reports; NTIA Indicators of Broadband Need

Income and Broadband Access

Household income is one of the strongest predictors of broadband adoption. Even when high-speed internet is available, lower-income households are less likely to subscribe due to cost barriers. This affordability gap means that access disparities persist not only between rural and urban areas, but also within cities and suburbs themselves.

Source: American Community Survey (ACS) 2023, Tables S1901 and S2801

Why This Gap Matters

Unequal broadband access shapes who can fully participate in modern society. Students without reliable internet face disadvantages in school, workers lose access to remote employment opportunities, patients struggle to use telehealth services, and communities are left out of civic life.

As governments invest billions of dollars in broadband expansion, understanding where gaps persist and what corporate motivations lie behind these gaps, is essential. Data-driven approaches can help ensure that new infrastructure investments reach the communities that need them most, rather than reinforcing existing inequalities.
